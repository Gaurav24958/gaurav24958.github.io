Life

"We do not learn from experience. We learn from reflecting on experience."

— John Dewey

Life isn't a sequence of achievements — it's a collection of moments that quietly shape who we become. Most of them don't seem significant while we're living them. A conversation, a failure, a journey, an unexpected opportunity, a decision made in uncertainty. Only in hindsight do we realize they changed everything.

A lot of my story is built on first's - things I did for the first time, earned for the first time, felt for the first time. Each one arrived at exactly the right moment, made possible by the work that quietly happened before it, and anchored by the unwavering support of my parents behind every one of them. Each first came with its own version of fear, curiosity, and the strange confidence that comes from simply deciding to try.

This page isn't meant to showcase accomplishments. It's a record of experiences that challenged my perspective, sparked my curiosity, and shaped the way I think about technology, people, and life. Some chapters are filled with excitement, others with setbacks, but every one of them contributed something I still carry.

As you scroll, you're not just moving through years - you're moving through the experiences that continue to shape the person I'm becoming. This isn't the story of where I've been. It's the story of how I got here, and why the next chapter matters even more.

Chapter 1

Childhood

Growing up, sitting still was impossible for me. I was a super active and notorious kid—always full of energy and up to some harmless mischief! Childhood was where many of my first's began. It was the first time curiosity really hit the kid in me - wanting to explore everything and figure out how the world worked around me. It was the first time I came across a camera, inspired by my dad who captured every single moment of our lives. Watching him freeze time with a camera taught me to appreciate life's small details. It was also the first time I fell in love with wheels. Back then, I was too small to ride real motorbikes, but I loved sitting behind my dad on rides, posing on parked bikes pretending to drive, and pedaling my bicycle like it was my dream motorcycle. That mix of non-stop energy, big bike dreams, and my very first moments behind a camera made my childhood unforgettable.

Chapter 2

High School

High school brought a surge of sports, numbers, and life-shaping adventures. I was always on the field—playing professional cricket (and carrying a broken finger to prove it!), alongside badminton and skating. Numbers were in my blood, a gift inherited from my mom and grandmother. Enrolling in Abacus and Vedic Maths, I loved playing with numbers and was convinced I was heading toward a career as a Chartered Accountant (spoiler: life had different plans!). I never had a lot of friends back then, so it was my dog Bruno who came into my life—he was, is, and will always be my world.

This was also when I took huge leaps toward independence. Thanks to my dad, who trusted me deeply and taught me how to be strong and enjoy doing things alone, I went on my first solo mountaineering course at just 15 years old. Scaling Shitidhar Peak at 15,600 feet became my first solo snow trek. That trek truly shaped me into a guy who can do things alone and stand on his own two feet. And to top off this exciting chapter, I finally experienced my first real bike ride—a huge milestone for the kid who used to pose on parked bikes.

Chapter 3

College

After high school, my path took an unexpected turn. I pivoted away from Chartered Accountancy to pursue engineering, giving everything I had to the IIT entrance exams. When I didn't qualify, heavy self-doubt hit me for the first time. But resetting my focus, I joined Pune Institute of Computer Technology to study Electronics - a decision that reshaped my entire perspective on life, setbacks, and human relationships.

Hostel life taught me what truly matters. True to who I am, I kept my circle small - always choosing quality over quantity - and found some constant friends who held me accountable, stood by me through ups and downs, and cheered for my goals. While still playing cricket, my energy poured into technology. I began winning hackathons, landed my first internship, and eventually secured my first job offer - working tirelessly to build a profile I could look back on with pride.

Chapter 4

Work

My first corporate job came with a learning curve nobody warned me about. Dragonfly Financial Technologies - later acquired by FIS - threw me into payments, compliance, and codebases I had never touched. The domain was foreign, the pace was different, and for a while I felt like I was constantly two steps behind everyone in the room. But I have always been able to catch up fast, and eventually the fog lifted and the work started making sense.

What nobody tells you about your first job is that the hardest part is rarely the work itself.

I was working from home. Alone between four walls, going from three years of college noise - people, hostels, late nights, constant motion - to near complete silence. The social isolation crept in slowly, then all at once. Anxiety, loneliness, boredom - three things that feel completely different but arrive together. What saved me was something my dad had quietly installed years earlier, without ever calling it preparation. The same man who put a 15-year-old on a mountaineering course alone, who believed that the only way to know you can stand on your own two feet is to actually be left standing on them - that upbringing is the only reason I did not fall apart. I started reading. Self-help first, then everything else. When you are alone enough, books stop being something you read and start being somewhere you go.

And then Bruno died.

My dog. My best friend since school. The one constant in a period that already felt unsteady - gone in a day, somewhere in the middle of a journey I was not ready to finish without him. I was not prepared for it. Grief is one of those things you think you understand until it is yours, and then you realise you understood nothing. It took a long time to accept and even longer to stop reaching for something that was no longer there.

But grief needed somewhere to go. I joined a gym. Started playing badminton again. Not for a body, not for health — but because I needed a space where I had to show up every single day and compete with the version of myself from yesterday. Discipline and consistency were not values I chose. They were things that grew out of loss because the alternative was staying still, and staying still felt worse.

Through all of this — the isolation, the grief, the slow rebuilding — I was laser focused on one thing running quietly in the background. I knew this chapter was not my destination. The work was good but the ceiling was visible, and I have never been comfortable with a ceiling I can see. I started building my master's applications. Got an acceptance from a university in the US, looked at the market, decided Europe made more sense for where I was heading. Applied only to UCD and TCD — two universities, no safety nets, because I have never seen the point in applying somewhere I would not be proud to attend. Somewhere in the middle of all of this I also sat for GATE Data Science, ranked AIR 596 out of 69,242 candidates, while working full time, while grieving, while writing applications at midnight. I travelled less. Explored less. Made sacrifices that hurt in the moment and made complete sense in hindsight.

Prolonged success asks for sacrifice. I have always believed that. When I look back at this chapter now, I am glad I never made it easy for myself.

The offer letter from Trinity College Dublin arrived and that was the end of this chapter and the beginning of the next. I had a GATE score that could have taken me to IIT Bombay. I chose TCD. Some decisions are simple when you know exactly what you are building toward.

Chapter 5

Ireland

The offer letter arrived on the 13th of January, 2026. I read it twice.

Trinity College Dublin. One of Europe's oldest universities. I had worked toward this for longer than I let most people know — through the isolation, through losing Bruno, through midnight application sessions after full days of work. When it finally came, it did not feel like relief. It felt like the starting gun.

At Trinity, I am going deep into Machine Learning, distributed systems, and advanced research in areas I spent my work years looking at from the outside. This is the theoretical foundation I always knew I was missing, and being here to build it properly, surrounded by centuries of scholarly history, feels like the right kind of hard. Not the grinding, surviving kind of hard. The kind where you are exactly where you are supposed to be and the difficulty is the point.

The best part? I will be the first member of my family to go abroad for higher education. For a kid who dreamt big, posing on parked bikes and pedaling a bicycle, getting ready to board the first flight of his life feels surreal.

I do not know exactly how this chapter ends. That is what makes it the best one so far.
